Bridging Chemistry and Experience

For decades, a gap has existed between the physical creation of a fragrance and the subjective experience it produces. Psychophysics is the science that bridges this gap. It provides a quantitative, predictive toolkit to understand how physical changes in a stimulus (like ingredient concentration) translate to changes in private, sensory perception. This is the bedrock of our experimental design approach.

The Fundamental Law of Sensation

The core of psychophysics is discovering the mathematical relationship between stimulus and sensation. Using a simple method called Magnitude Estimation, where people assign numbers to reflect perceived intensity, a universal pattern emerges: Stevens' Power Law.

Stevens' Power Law: S = kI‚Åø

This equation shows that perceived sensation (S) equals physical intensity (I) raised to an exponent (n). This exponent reveals everything.

Understanding this law is the first step. For fragrance, it tells us our perception of scent is not linear. A scent that is twice as concentrated is not perceived as twice as strong. This non-linearity can be precisely measured and predicted, forming the basis for rational product design.

Compressive (n < 1)

Large changes in concentration produce small changes in perceived intensity. This is true for most odors.

Linear (n = 1)

Perceived intensity grows in direct proportion to physical intensity, like judging the length of a line.

Expansive (n > 1)

Small changes in the stimulus produce large changes in sensation, like the pain from an electric shock.

Quantifying Fragrance Ingredients

By applying this science, we can create a dose-response curve for any fragrance ingredient. This allows us to predict how a consumer will perceive changes in a formula before it's even made, turning product development from an art into a predictive science.

Dose-Response Curves for Odor & Liking

Notice how intensity (solid line) for all ingredients is compressive (the curve flattens), while liking (dashed line) behaves differently for each one. We can now see the precise point where adding more of a pleasant ingredient like Ethyl Vanillin stops adding value, or where an unpleasant note like Indole becomes intolerable.

The Complexity of Blending

Fragrances are not simple additions of their parts. The presence of one ingredient can dramatically change the perception of another, often by suppressing its character. Psychophysics allows us to measure and model these complex interactions, moving beyond simple ingredient studies to understand the fragrance as a whole.

Odor Suppression in a Mixture

As we add more of the minty Pulegone, the perceived intensity of the citrusy Limonene is suppressed, even when the physical amount of Limonene remains high. This is why blending requires science, not just intuition. Understanding these foundational principles of perception is the first step. The next is to apply this rigorous experimental design to complete fragrance accords to map the senses of the consumer.

The Scarlet Thread of Our Science

Many researchers test products. Very few run experiments. This distinction is the single most important differentiator of our work. Standard methods test a few finished products to find a winner. We systematically explore a wide space of possibilities to discover the rules that govern what people love.

The Four Steps of Experimental Design

Our process is a systematic journey from deconstruction to discovery.

STEP 1

Deconstruct into Elements

We begin by breaking down a product or concept into its fundamental building blocks. These elements are the independent variables in our experiment. They can be sensory accords (e.g., 'Spicy Note'), descriptive phrases ('A tangy blend of fruit...'), functional benefits ('Leaves skin softer'), or even brand names.

STEP 2

Systematic Combination (The Vignettes)

We create hundreds of unique combinations (vignettes) from these elements. Crucially, the underlying design is orthogonal, meaning each element is tested independently and appears an equal number of times. Each respondent evaluates a different, systematically incomplete set of these vignettes, ensuring we cover the entire experimental space and get unbiased reads on every single element.

STEP 3

Individual Modeling (N of 1)

This is the key. Because each person rates many unique combinations, we can run a regression analysis for every single respondent. This gives us a personal equation (e.g., Liking = Base + 15*ElementA - 5*ElementB...) that describes what drives liking for that one person. We move beyond averages to capture individual truth.

STEP 4

Cluster to Find Mindsets

With a database of individual preference "equations," we can now use cluster analysis to find groups of people who share the same patterns of liking and disliking. These are the true, data-driven mindsets‚Äîgroups of people who respond to the world in similar ways, waiting to be discovered.

Mapping the Senses

A scientific method for segmenting consumers based on their response to physical fragrance accords.

From Ingredients to Experiences

Knowing how individual ingredients behave is the foundation. The next step is to understand how people perceive complete fragrances. The challenge is that fragrance preference is deeply personal, yet R&D and marketing decisions are often based on averages. This leads to missed opportunities and launches that don't deeply resonate.

The Proof: A Foundational 1986 Case Study

This methodology's power was first demonstrated in a landmark 1986 paper by Howard Moskowitz. By systematically varying accords like Sweetness, Floweriness, and Spiciness, the study uncovered three distinct preference segments hidden within the "average" consumer response.

How Liking Changes by Accord and Segment

The chart reveals the critical insight: the "average" response is a fiction. In reality, there are distinct groups with often opposing preferences. For Spiciness, Mindset 3's liking grows, while Mindset 1's plummets. Standard research would average these out and conclude "no effect," missing the opportunity entirely.

The Three Sensory Mindsets

The analysis uncovered three "mindsets," not based on who they are, but on what they sensorially prefer. These are the true, hidden drivers of choice.

Mindset 1

Dislikes strong, heavy notes. Prefers fragrances that are subtle and fresh. Their liking drops as intensity increases.

Mindset 2

The "classic" fragrance lover. They seek balance and enjoy sweet and floral accords, but only up to a certain point before it becomes too much.

Mindset 3

Wants to make a statement. Their liking grows with the intensity of heavy, spicy, and strong accords. For them, more is more.

Identifying these fundamental sensory mindsets is half the story. It tells us *what* people like. But to truly connect, we also need to understand the language that excites them. The same experimental design principles can be applied to the world of ideas, words, and concepts.

Mapping the Mind

The Power of Words

Beyond the physical scent, the way we talk about a fragrance powerfully shapes perception and preference. We apply the same experimental design principles to deconstruct the ideas behind a fragrance, revealing how specific words, benefits, and emotions drive consumer interest. This allows us to build concepts and messaging that resonate with the mind of the consumer.

Deconstructing the Fragrance Concept

The Importance of Odor Description

How much of a concept's appeal comes from the scent description alone? Our experimental approach can quantify this precisely. The answer varies dramatically by product category, revealing exactly where to focus your messaging.

Contribution of Scent Description to Total Appeal

For a personal product like "Summer Fragrance," the description is almost everything (86% of the appeal). For a functional cleaner, it's far less important. This data provides a clear guide for marketing and communication strategy.

Conceptual Mindsets: The Voice of the Consumer

This conceptual analysis reveals new segments based on how people react to fragrance ideas. These are different from sensory segments and provide a new layer of insight for marketing and concept development.

Mindset 1

They are excited by descriptive, evocative fragrance language. For them, the story of the scent is a primary driver of interest. They have a low base interest and need the elements to be convinced.

Mindset 2

They have a high base interest in the product category but are not swayed much by specific fragrance descriptions. They focus on functional benefits and brand trust.

Mindset 3

This group is turned off by detailed fragrance descriptions. They have a high base interest but prefer concepts focused purely on function and performance, with no mention of scent.

Case Study: Creating the Optimal "Summer Fragrance" Concept

The optimal concept is different for each mind-set. Mindset 1 wants rich descriptions, while Mindset 3 wants no scent description at all. Knowing this allows for targeted messaging that resonates, instead of one average message that fails to excite anyone.

Creating Your Fragrance Encyclopedia

The next step is to replicate this powerful study for your specific domain. By creating a database that links language to different end-uses and mindsets, we can build a predictive engine for concept creation.

1. Define End-Uses

What are the specific situations or need-states we want to own? (e.g., "Relaxing before bed," "Waking up refreshed," "Focusing on a task").

Goal: ~15 distinct end-uses.

2. Create the Language

What are all the words and phrases that paint a picture of the desired experience? (e.g., "Drift away on a cloud of calm," "A burst of morning citrus").

Goal: ~24 "word picture" phrases.

3. Define the Aromas

What are the key fragrance elements, benefits, and ingredients we want to test? (e.g., "With calming lavender," "Infused with energizing mint").

Goal: ~16 aroma & benefit elements.
